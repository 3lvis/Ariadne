# Ariadne

In Greek myth, Ariadne gives Theseus the thread that lets him enter the labyrinth, reach the center, and find his way back out.

## The Problem

Modern software is extraordinarily good at helping us move through the world until we reach a door.

It can route us across cities, continents, and oceans. It can predict traffic, estimate arrivals, and redraw a journey in real time. But the moment we step into an unfamiliar building, that intelligence often disappears. The final stretch becomes uncertain again. We slow down. We look around. We interrupt someone. We guess.

This is the gap Ariadne exists to close.

Buildings are still full of invisible complexity. Offices become mazes over time. Hospitals, campuses, hotels, transit hubs, and public venues all present the same quiet problem: once you are inside, how do you know where to go next?

## The Idea

Ariadne starts from a simple belief: indoor navigation should feel less like interpreting a diagram and more like being guided.

The best version of this experience is calm and direct:

1. You arrive.
2. The space is recognized.
3. You choose where you want to go.
4. The path becomes clear.

## The Vision

Ariadne aims to make indoor spaces legible.

In a legible building, the space does not feel mute. An entrance means something. A hallway is the thread towards your destination. Software understands enough about where you are, where you intend to go, through this knowledege the environment begins to explain itself.

Through this, Ariadne becomes more than a navigation tool. It becomes a new interface for place itself.

That future could include:

- arrival experiences that work the moment you enter a building
- collaborative spatial understanding across trusted people and devices
- accessibility experiences built around explicit indoor guidance
- context-aware interactions that begin with place and expand into memory, task, and presence

## The Standard

Ariadne should feel inevitable in use.

The best products of this kind do not feel technical even when the underlying work is enormously complex. They feel obvious, calm, and trustworthy. They remove hesitation instead of adding novelty.

That means Ariadne should be:

- direct
- quiet
- forgiving
- precise

It should guide without becoming noisy. It should recover gracefully when the world does not cooperate. It should feel native to the human act of moving through space.

## The Horizon

Outdoor navigation changed how people experience cities. It changed what felt reachable, knowable, and easy.

Indoor orientation is still waiting for that shift.

The long horizon for Ariadne is a spatial layer for the buildings people move through every day: workplaces, institutions, public venues, and shared environments that should be easier to understand the moment you enter them.

If outdoor maps taught software how to understand roads, Ariadne is software that understand arrival, intent, and movement inside buildings.
